Syllabus for SWE 622 - Distributed Software Engineering - Fall 2017

What is this course?

This course conveys key concepts for designing and building distributed software systems.  The course is geared towards software engineers that work mostly at the application-level, but need to understand the features and limitations of existing middleware for distributed systems.  Additionally, the course covers some research topics related to currently open problems. Specifically, some of the topic that will be covered are as follows: definition and scope of distribution, principles of communication and computation, socket programming, remote method invocation, software architecture of distributed systems, middleware systems, service discovery, quality of service, adaptive systems, mobility, security, and fault tolerance. Reading List

The textbook is Distributed Systems: Principles and Paradigms (2nd edition), Andrew S. Tanenbaum and Maarten Van Steen, 2007/2016, ISBN 0-13-239227-5. Either the hardback or paperback is fine. You can get a free personal digital copy of the second edition if you send the authors your email address. Beware: There's also a third edition that was released in 2017; we're not using that edition. See below for supplemental papers.

I may add a few additional required reading articles as we go.

The book is fine for fundamentals, but we will supplement it with some key additions. This includes discussions of REST, Blockchains (including Bitcoin), and Apache Hadoop and Spark.

Grading

  1. Five Reading Assignments 20% (5 x 4%)
  2. Two Programming Assignments 30% (2 x 15%)
  3. Two Exams 48% (2 x 24%)
  4. Hadoop/Spark online quiz (2%)

The exams are closed book, closed notes (no access to either), no Internet. You may bring a numeric-only calculator (one with a log function is convenient), but not one which can store texts or has Internet access.

The reading assignments are group assignments, with open book, open notes, and open Internet. I want groups of 4, though some variation is possible. List your group members in your submission. Groups do not have to have the same membership each time (though they often do, and that's fine too). Everyone must contribute to the group. It's an honor code violation if someone OUTSIDE the group does your work. If there is disagreement, work to figure out the cause, so that you can produce better answers. For example, some answers may vary depending on certain factors; your group must figure out what factors the answer depends on.

The programming assignments are individual assignments; you must do your own work.

Assignments must be submitted via Blackboard unless otherwise warranted. For group assignments (the reading assignments) only one team member needs to submit. I typically use tools to check for plagerism; please don't plagerize. Do not turn them in late; penalty is -10% per day, and not accepted after three days. Students must give me notice as soon as reasonably possible if an illness or other unexpected event will make it impossible to submit work or take a test on time.

I grade on results, not effort. Please don't tell me "I worked so hard" as a reason to improve your grade; I presume everyone in the class works hard, and it's not fair to improve your grade if others produced better results.

Academic Integrity (Honor Code)

GMU is an Honor Code university; it's baked into the GMU culture. I take the honor code very seriously.

It is expected that students adhere to the George Mason University Honor Code as it relates to integrity regarding coursework and grades. The Honor Code reads as follows: "To promote a stronger sense of mutual responsibility, respect, trust, and fairness among all members of the George Mason University community and with the desire for greater academic and personal achievement, we, the student members of the University Community have set forth this: Student members of the George Mason University community pledge not to cheat, plagiarize, steal and/or lie in matters related to academic work." More information about the Honor Code, including definitions of cheating, lying, and plagiarism, can be found at the Office of Academic Integrity website at http://oai.gmu.edu.

In particular, when you are responsible for a task, you will perform that task. When you rely on someone else’s work in an aspect of the performance of that task, you will give full credit in the proper, accepted form. Another aspect of academic integrity is the free play of ideas. Vigorous discussion and debate are encouraged in this course, with the firm expectation that all aspects of the class will be conducted with civility and respect for differing ideas, perspectives, and traditions. When in doubt (of any kind) please ask for guidance and clarification. Do not plagiarize, and do not enable others to plagiarize from you.

Although students are encouraged to discuss the topics covered in class, all homework assignments (reading assignments), exams, and projects (programming assignments) are to be completed individually, unless joint work is explicitly authorized by the instructor. If joint work is authorized, all contributing students must be listed on the submission.

Failure to adhere to the honor code can result in a variety of sanctions, including a zero on the assignment, an F in the course, suspension, or expulsion from GMU. Harsher sanctions are normal for graduate students; we expect at this point that you know better.

See the Computer Science Honor Code policies for more.
